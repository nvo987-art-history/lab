Research

This work is based on a single premise: color cannot be reduced to an isolated parameter without losing its structural role. Within relational systems, color does not function as a property applied to form, but as a condition through which form emerges.

The foundation document develops this position by examining the difference between painterly color and algorithmic color representation. In painting, color operates as a system of dependencies in which each element is defined through its relation to others. No value remains stable or independent, and any modification affects the entire structure.

Algorithmic models, by contrast, treat color as a set of discrete variables. They assume that visual outcomes can be constructed through controlled manipulation of independent parameters. This approach fails when applied to relational color systems, where interaction cannot be decomposed into separable components.

The problem is not a lack of precision, but a mismatch of structure. Relational color behavior cannot be fully translated into systems that rely on fixed values and local operations. As a result, algorithmic representations are unable to reproduce the generative role of color observed in painterly practice.

The research focuses on identifying this limitation, analyzing its structure, and constructing situations in which it becomes observable. The objective is not to simulate painterly effects, but to understand why such systems resist formalization.
