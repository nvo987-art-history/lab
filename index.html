Foundation

This research is grounded in the assumption that color cannot be understood as an isolated visual parameter. Instead, it operates as a relational system in which each element is defined through its interaction with others. The foundation document establishes this position and examines the structural consequences of treating color as a generative rather than descriptive component.

Within painterly systems, color does not fill form but actively produces it. Form emerges through chromatic interaction, meaning that spatial and structural relationships are inseparable from color behavior. This stands in contrast to computational approaches where form is predefined and color is applied as a secondary attribute.

The document defines the conceptual boundary between relational color systems and algorithmic representation, forming the basis for further analysis and experimentation.

Relational Color Structure

In a relational system, no color exists independently. Each value is continuously modified by its surrounding elements, producing a dynamic structure that cannot be reduced to fixed parameters.

Color relationships are not additive or modular. Any modification propagates through the entire system, altering the structure as a whole. This behavior prevents decomposition into isolated components.

The research focuses on describing these dependencies and understanding color as a field of interaction rather than a collection of values.

Algorithmic Limitation

Algorithmic systems typically represent color as a set of discrete values that can be independently controlled and recombined. This approach assumes that visual structures can be constructed through parameter manipulation.

When applied to relational color systems, this model fails. Because color is not independent, parameter-based control cannot reproduce the structural coherence observed in painterly systems.

The limitation is therefore conceptual rather than technical, arising from the mismatch between relational behavior and discrete representation.

Generative Behavior

Color is treated as a generative mechanism rather than a descriptive layer. It actively produces spatial organization and visual structure through interaction and contrast.

This generative behavior cannot be reduced to static values or predefined rules. It emerges through dynamic relations, perception, and temporal change.

The research investigates how such behavior can be observed and partially reconstructed without collapsing it into simplified models.

Ongoing Work

  • Analysis of relational dependencies in color systems
  • Comparison with parameter-based algorithmic models
  • Development of experimental color configurations
  • Documentation of structural observations

Lab Process

This lab functions as a continuous record of the research process. Materials are added progressively as the work develops, including notes, experiments, and intermediate results.

The objective is to build a coherent body of work reflecting the evolving understanding of color as a relational system.
